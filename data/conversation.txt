DRIVER: No, not tonight. I need to do my logs, get some rest. I can roll tomorrow morning, probably seven, seven-thirty. Call it available tomorrow morning.

DISPATCHER: Got it, tomorrow morning. Now I want to get you something good before we start throwing stuff at you. You're still in the fifty-three dry van, right?

DRIVER: Correct. Fifty-three foot dry van, that's what I run. Solo, not team — I don't do team driving. Never have.

DISPATCHER: And weight-wise — what's your max?

DRIVER: Forty-four thousand. I had a broker try to slip me a forty-seven-thousand-pound load last month, said it was "close enough." I said no. Forty-four is my limit. I won't go over.

DISPATCHER: Smart. What about hazmat? You certified?

DRIVER: No hazmat. I keep meaning to get it but never got around to it. So no, not certified.

DISPATCHER: Okay. Now the big question — rate. What do you need to make it worth getting out of bed?

DRIVER: Look, I've been doing this long enough to know the old per-mile rate is a trap. I need at least two-fifty a mile, and I mean all-in effective — that means when you add up the deadhead to get to the pick-up, the loaded miles, and the deadhead back to get me home, divide the price by ALL of that. I had a load a few weeks back that was paying three bucks a mile loaded, but by the time I drove two hundred empty miles to the origin and then dropped in the middle of nowhere, it worked out to like a buck forty effective. I can't do that.

DISPATCHER: No, that's totally fair. Two-fifty all-in effective, we'll make sure we're calculating it that way. Now where are you trying to end up? You home-based in Memphis still?

DRIVER: Yeah, Memphis. My wife and kids are there, I try to get back weekends when I can. I don't always make it but that's the goal. Memphis is home base.

DISPATCHER: And in terms of where you're willing to run — any preferences on lanes or regions?

DRIVER: I like the Southeast, honestly. Tennessee, Georgia, the Carolinas, Alabama — that area works great for me because it brings me toward home. I'll do Midwest too, like Missouri, Illinois, that's fine. What I don't want is to go out West. I don't want to go to Colorado or past that. I've had loads to California and it takes a week to get back — I hate it. No disrespect but I avoid the West Coast like the plague.

DISPATCHER: Makes total sense. Anything else I should flag in your profile?

DRIVER: A couple things. I don't do livestock. I'm not set up for that, and honestly I just don't want to deal with it. And no oversized loads — I don't have the permits for oversized freight. Just standard dry freight, boxes, pallets, whatever. And one more thing — I don't want to be out more than five days if I can help it. I can stretch to a week if the money is really right, but I'm not looking to be out two weeks straight. Five days is my preference.

DISPATCHER: Got it. So let me read this back — you're in Dallas right now at the Pilot on I-20, available tomorrow morning. Fifty-three dry van, solo, no team. Max weight forty-four thousand. Two-fifty a mile all-in effective minimum. Home-based in Memphis. Southeast and Midwest preferred, no West Coast. No hazmat, no livestock, no oversized, and five days out preferred. That about cover it?

DRIVER: Yeah, that's it. You've got it.
